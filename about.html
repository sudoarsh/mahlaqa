ماہ لقا بائی چندا — ایک تعارف

The First Woman to Compose a Complete Diwan in Urdu

Mah Laqa Bai Chanda was born around 1768 in Hyderabad and died in 1820/21, most likely from the cholera epidemic that swept the Deccan during those years — a fact recorded in Tarikh-e-Gulzar-e-Asifiya and not previously noted in published scholarship.

She was the first woman to compose a complete diwan in Urdu. This distinction is not merely a historical footnote. It places her at the origin point of a tradition of women's formal literary production in Urdu that has continued for two centuries since.

She was trained in music by Khushhal Khan, a direct descendant of Tansen — the legendary musician of Akbar's court — making her musical lineage among the most prestigious in the Hindustani classical tradition. She was trained in poetry by Sher Muhammad Khan Iman, one of the significant poets of the Hyderabadi school. Iman died in 1806, twelve years before the Salar Jung manuscript was completed.

She occupied a central position in the court of Nizam Ali Khan and his successors. Her deodhi in Mandi Mir Alam stood adjacent to that of Mir Alam, the most powerful minister of the Nizam's court. In 1799 she gifted a manuscript of her diwan to James Kirkpatrick, the British Resident — a political act as much as a literary one, occurring in the same year that Kirkpatrick documented her in his letters as already famous across the court.

She received the title Mah Laqa Bai — Moon-Faced Lady — from Nizam II, most probably at the Nauroz durbar of 1802. The Nizam died in 1803. One ghazal in the Salar Jung manuscript, absent from the earlier 1799 manuscript and addressed to the Nizam at Nauroz, was almost certainly composed at this occasion. It may be the poem that prompted the title's conferral.

In 1818 she commissioned a history of Hyderabad — Tarikh-e-Dil-Afroz, written by Ghulam Husain Khan Jauhar — and had her diwan appended to it as a permanent historical record. A woman writing herself into the historical record of her city, six years before her death. The Salar Jung manuscript is that appended diwan.

Her tomb stands on the Maula Ali hill in Hyderabad, still visited today. The ashurkhana of her deodhi survives in a street in Mandi Mir Alam. The mosque of her music teacher Khushhal Khan stands nearby. These sites were documented and photographed in March 2026 for this edition.
